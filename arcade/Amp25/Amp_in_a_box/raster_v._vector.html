Raster versus vector

 

All TVs and most video games are raster-based, that is, the image is drawn on screen by starting in the upper left corner, moving the electron beam across the screen and lighting up the different colored phosphor dots along the way at the right intensity. When the beam gets to the right edge of the screen it ‘flies back’ to the left edge, also moving one scan line down, and repeats the process. Once the entire screen is filled the beam (now in the lower right corner) ‘retraces’ its path to the upper left and repeats the process. The beam, emitted from the narrow neck part of the tube, is deflected into position by the magnetic fields generated by the yoke that straddles the tube. There’s a coil of fine wire wound into a electromagnet that is responsible for the horizontal movement, and another that does the vertical.

This all happens very fast - fast enough that the beam has started drawing a new frame before the old one has faded. The source of the signal has to be fast enough to keep up if all the dots of the screen are to be filled. Television accomplished this like radio - by using (well, used to use) analog waves of information broadcast over the air. The TV would tune in to a certain frequency (channel) of waves, amplify the signal and pass it on to the electron beam. The creation of the image was handled by the camera or tape machine, with relatively little processing along the way. Hard to edit, but easier to transmit and display.

A 1980s arcade game board attempting to create an image from scratch (and then update it several times per second) could not handle that much work. The solution was to create less information, and that’s why early games are low-resolution games - the beam’s position changes more slowly, and fewer dots are drawn.

A vector arcade game overcomes low resolution by not attempting to fill the entire screen. Only the edges of objects are drawn, and the blank areas are simply ignored. Instead of traversing the entire screen row by row, line by line, the beam of electrons only has to be deflected from point to relevant point. Resolution is limited only by the range of horizontal (X) and vertical (Y) values programmed into the game, and by the density (or pitch) of the phosphor dots on the coating of the picture tube.

The different demands of moving the beam in this manner necessitate a different kind of yoke. Instead of the constant seesaw of the horizontal part of a raster yoke, a vector yoke requires a more refined, pinpoint approach in both X and Y axes.
